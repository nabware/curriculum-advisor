Study Guide – Week 7: Post-Training & Forgetting
The week is focusing on post-training (continual learning), catastrophic forgetting, safety
degradation, and mitigation strategies such as replay and self-rehearsal.




1. What Is Post-Training?
Post-training refers to further training after:
- Pre-training (foundation model, after phase 1)
- Alignment (SFT(phase 2) + RLHF(phase 3))
Goal: > Improve performance on a target task or domain.
Examples: - Teach Spanish - Improve programming - Add speech understanding
Post-training can use:
- Pre-train style (methods in phase 1 training, training data: e.g., articles, books, etc.)
- SFT style (methods in phase 2 training, training data: question and answer pairs )
- RL style (methods in phase 3 training, training data: Q&A pairs and human feedback)
depending on the training dataset you have.




2. Post-Training: Foundation Model → Fine-Tuned Model
Pipeline: Foundation Model → Alignment → Fine-Tuned Model
Important: Post-training enhances target capability, but may degrade previous capabilities
Trade-off is unavoidable.




3. Case Study: Teaching LLaMA-2-Chat Spanish
Observation: - Continued pre-training on Spanish - Model learns Spanish responses - But
alignment/safety behavior may degrade
Key insight: > Post-training can override alignment.
Safety loss is real.




4. Post-Training Can Break Safety
Research shows: - Fine-tuning aligned models may compromise safety - Even if user intent
is not malicious
Alignment is fragile.




5. Case study: Speech + Multimodal Extension
Example: Teaching LLaMA to understand speech:
- Add speech encoder + adapter
- Fine-tune on speech tasks
Observation: - Original text formatting abilities (e.g., JSON output) may persist - But careful
training required




6. Catastrophic Forgetting
Definition: > Model loses performance on previous tasks after learning new ones.
Analogy from lecture: “The surgery succeeded, but the patient died.”
Post-training is like brain surgery.




7. Scaling Does NOT Solve Forgetting
Empirical findings: - Larger models (1B–7B) still forget - Forgetting severity does not vanish
with scale
Bigger ≠ immune.
8. LoRA Learns Less and Forgets Less
LoRA (adapter-based tuning): - Updates small number of parameters - Preserves more
original knowledge
Trade-off: - Less capacity shift - Less forgetting




9. Old Continual Learning Solutions: Experience Replay
   •   Mix small portion of old data, even 5% can help
Experience replay is highly effective — IF old data is available.




10. Problem: No Access to Old Training Data
In practice: - We do not have original pretraining data - Cannot store massive internet
corpus - Cannot access proprietary alignment data
So classic replay is difficult.


       • Solution #1: Self-Generated Experience Replay
       Solution idea: - Use the model to regenerate old-style data - Replay synthetic
       samples
       Process: 1. Generate training examples 2. Mix with new task data 3. Fine-tune
       Called: - Self-generated replay - Pseudo experience replay


       • Solution #2: Paraphrasing & Self-Rehearsal
       Advanced approach: - Ask model to rephrase previous outputs - Keep only correct
       outputs - Selectively rehearse
       Selective Self-Rehearsal: - Use AI-generated outputs carefully - Avoid reinforcing
       errors
       • Another mitigation of forgetting: Limit difficult token
         training (learn less and forget less)
       Example: “Long time no see”
       Early tokens: - Hard to predict full phrase - Sequence-level knowledge matters
       Continual learning must preserve: - Phrase-level knowledge - Long-range
       dependencies




11. Core Trade-Off of Post-Training
Post-training: - Improves target performance - Risks catastrophic forgetting - Risks
alignment degradation
Mitigation: - Replay - Regularization - Adapter-based tuning - Self-rehearsal
No perfect solution.
