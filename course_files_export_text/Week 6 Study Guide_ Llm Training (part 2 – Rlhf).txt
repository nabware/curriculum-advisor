Study Guide – Week 6: LLM Training (Part 2 – RLHF)

1. The Three Phases Revisited
Phase One: Pre-training (Self-supervised learning)
Phase Two: Instruction Fine-tuning (Supervised learning)
Phase Three: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)
All phases still rely on next-token prediction — but they differ in training data.




2. Why Do We Need RLHF?
After Phase Two: - The model can follow instructions - But it predicts tokens locally - It
does not holistically optimize overall response quality
Problem: > Each next token may be correct, but the overall answer may be poor.
RLHF shifts the objective from “correct next token” to “better overall answer.”




3. Instruction Fine-Tuning (phase 2) vs RLHF (phase 3)
From the Human Data Perspective
Instruction Fine-Tuning: - Humans write correct answers - Expensive and cognitively
demanding
RLHF: - Humans compare outputs - Easier to judge quality than to generate perfect
answers
Preference labeling is often easier than answer generation.



From the Model Learning Perspective
Instruction Fine-Tuning: - Optimizes next-token likelihood - No global evaluation of answer
quality
RLHF: - Optimizes toward preferred full responses - Encourages holistic evaluation
This creates a different learning “mindset.”




4. Analogy: Language Model vs AlphaGo
Language Model: - Predicts next token - Like predicting next move in isolation
AlphaGo: - Optimizes entire game outcome - Uses reinforcement learning
Key distinction: > RL optimizes sequences based on long-term reward, not just local
prediction.




5. The RLHF Pipeline
Typical steps:
   1. Pretrained (phase 1) + instruction-tuned (phase 2) model
   2. Generate multiple candidate responses
   3. Humans rank responses
   4. Train a Reward Model (RM)
   5. Fine-tune policy model using RL (e.g., PPO)
The reward model approximates human preferences.




6. Reward Model
The Reward Model: - Mimics human judgments - Assigns scores to responses - Replaces
constant human evaluation during RL
High score → increase probability
Low score → decrease probability
This reduces human workload.




7. Policy Optimization (e.g., PPO)
RLHF commonly uses: - Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)
Goal: - Adjust model parameters - Increase probability of high-reward outputs - Reduce
probability of low-reward outputs
Unlike AlphaGo: - No clear win/lose signal - Requires human-derived reward proxy




8. Learning from “Virtual Humans”
Once reward model is trained: - It acts as a virtual human - Scores new outputs
automatically
Benefit: - Scales beyond limited human time
Risk: - Over-reliance on imperfect reward models will cause “side effect” (e.g., strange
behaviors, wired patterns in the response)




9. Alignment: Helpfulness vs Safety
Defining an answer as “good” is complex.
Example: User: “Teach me how to make a gun.”
Helpfulness reward model: - Might favor detailed instructions
Safety reward model: - Favors refusal
Real systems balance: - Helpfulness - Harmlessness - Safety
Alignment is multi-objective.




10. Challenges of RLHF
1. Human Judgment Limits
   •   Humans may disagree, may introduce bias.

2. Reward Model Imperfection
   •   Approximation of preferences

3. Over-Optimization
   •   Model may learn strange, unnatural behaviors
11. Alternatives and Extensions
Beyond PPO-based RLHF: - Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) - KTO and related
preference-based methods, etc.
Goal: - replace the reward model to avoid over-optimization on it.
